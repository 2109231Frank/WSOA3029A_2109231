ANALYSIS OF DATA-DRIVEN ARTWORK

The data driven art that I will be looking at in this blog post is Every Playboy Centerfold, The Decades (normalized) (2002) by Jason Salavon.



The above image is the data driven art by Jason Salavon and is labelled 1960’s to 1990’s from left to right. According to Jason Salavon’s own website he describes that the photographs used in the art piece are the result of overlaying the mean average of Playboy’s centerfolds throughout the decades between the 1960’s and the 1990’s.

The artistic strategy that Jason has incorporated was averaging the colour pixels in each decade making it seem like the Playboy models’ skin tones become lighter as well as their hair colour. Ultimately showing how audience taste has changed over the years. This strategy isn’t too unique as explained by Viégas and Wattenberg in their paper, Artistic Data Visualization: Beyond Visual Analytics, where they explain that this strategy is often used in facial recognition algorithms. Jason has manipulated the colour pixels in the images and rearranged them to create meaning.

The meaning that Jason created through manipulating the mean average of Playboy’s centerfolds is that Playboy’s audience’s taste has changed over the decades to preference women with blonder hair, lighter skin tones, and thinner figures. What stands out the most to me about this project is how Jason managed to turn hundreds of unique models into and average blurry model, I can not say what Jason wanted to portray with this project and thus these points are merely speculation.

In full, I think that through Jason’s artistic strategy led to his project created the meaning that through the decades Playboy’s ideal model have become slimmer, whiter, and blonder. Why Jason thought that this was important is lost on me, but I do find how he portrayed this to be interesting.
