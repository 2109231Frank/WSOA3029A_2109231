Data visualization is the process of changing raw data into information that is represented in a visual form. This data allows viewers to see and understand the patterns and relationships within data which, in turn, creates the possibility of informed decision making and informed predictions of future data. There are many definitions of data visualisation, but all of them include, to some extent, what I have said above. Data visualization has been around for as long as human beings have been recording history and is essential for understanding raw data and what that data means. This has resulted in a massive amount of invention and discovery throughout history (Crapo, Waisel, Wallace, & Willemain, 2000). Since computer technologies have been introduced into the equation, data visualisation has become a lot easier to manifest since we no longer have to hand draw charts etc. to represent data: instead, we can use algorithms and various computer software to do this for us (Friendly, 2009). Traditional data visualisation is focused on the communication for understanding data rather than any other approaches, and this type of data visualisation has a number of advantages. These advantages include:
  • It has the ability to visually represent a vast amount of data immediately.
  • It enables viewers or analysts to immediately view emergent patterns within the data which allows them to formulate new insights.
  • It can be used for product quality control seeing that the identification of problems are revealed almost immediately through data analysis.
  • It improves the understanding of large- and small-scale data.

The reason for the importance of data visualisation is, simply put, that it allows us to see and understand patterns and relationships within data, which is crucial to a variety of important decisions within businesses etc. Data and information go hand in hand and the definitions on both of these concepts vary, but generally “data” refers to information that is raw and unprocessed. “information” refers to processed and usable data.There are two forms of data, namely: primary and secondary data. Primary data refer to data that is collected directly from the units or individual correspondents for the purpose of information studies. Secondary data is information within data which is processed or analysed in order to extract information for various purposes (Agarwal, 2006, p3). From the above information we can assume that primary data is raw data and secondary data is information. There are many forms or techniques of data visualisation that depend on the data and its form. It is up to the data and their form to dictate which type of visualisation will be used in order to analyse said data.Ultimately data visualisation has been around for a very long time and is as important as ever. The only changes to data visualisation that have ben made and probably will be made in the future is how it is data is processed and analysed in order to create information that can be used in a visual form. These visual forms are dictated by the type of data that is being used and there are many to choose from.
